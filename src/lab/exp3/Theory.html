EPR as a Tool To Investigate the Transition Metal Complexes

When a molecule or compound with an unpaired electron is placed in a strong magnetic field, the spin of the unpaired electron can align in two different ways creating two spin states, ms = ± ½. The alignment can either be along the direction (parallel) to the magnetic filed which corresponds to the lower energy state ms = - ½ or opposite (antiparallel) to the direction of the applied magnetic field ms = + ½ . The two alignments have different energies and this difference in energy lifts the degeneracy of the electron spin states.

Picture source: http://hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu/hbase/molecule/esr.html

Energy used:
Microwave energy is used for the irradiation of the sample. Microwave region around 9.4 Ghz (~ 3cm wavelength) known as X-band is used mostly for it shows high sensitivity for a workable sample size. Other frequencies that are used are listed below:

Sample preparation
1. Sample tubes: High purity quartz tubes are used (glass tubes show paramagnetic impurities and so are not used in EPR measurements).
For non polar solvent, cylindrical tubes with inner diameter of 3 to 4 mm are used.
For polar solvents capillary tubes or flat cells are used (to reduce the dielectric loss).
2. Temperature: Transition metals are recorded at room temperature and at low temperature (4 -100 K).
3. Concentration: Concentration of the sample to be measured depends on the spectrum broadness. Broad spectra requires higher concentration.

Conc. ∝ Amp. x (Width)2








Free radicals show narrow lines and low concentration of 10-7 M is good enough for a strong spectrum. Metalloproteins show broad spectrum Particularly when S>1/2 and high concentration of 10-3 to 10-5 M of the sample is required.

Paramater settings:
Parameters optimization is done to get the best signal to noise ratio and it is unique for each sample. The 'g value' ranges from 1.8 to 9. Some of the settings to get strong signal for the transition metal complexes are:
(i) lowering the temperature of the sample. Temperature is lowered from 4 to 100K depending on the oxidation and spin state.
(ii) Microwave power around 20mw.
(iiI)modulation amplitude around 10G (1.0mT).
(iv) Magnetic field from 1000 to 4000 Gauss.
(v)Time constant (moderate) no greater than 0.1 second.
(vi) scan rate (multiple) ~ 5 minutes scan.

EPR spectra:
It is the first derivative of the absorption spectra (instrument artifact), to increase the sensitivity of the spectrometer the magnetic field is modulated.

Signal amplitude / Magnetic filed = first derivative of absorption signal















The electron paramagnetic resonance of metalloproteins. Biochemical Society Transactions [1985, 13(3):548-60] Palmer G

Advantages of EPR measurements:
Useful in determining the structure domain in which the paramagnet resides. The fundamental property measured in an EPR experiment is the ' g value' which indirectly gives the magnetic moment (µ) of the substance. EPR has many advantages over magnetic susceptibility measurements:
(i) EPR looks at the individual species and is not a bulk method.
(ii) Looks at the interaction between the paramagnetic centre and the neighboring nuclei
(iii) EPR is 1000 fold more sensitive
